\section{Field and Galois Theory}


To study the structure of the field, consider a natural map: Let \(K\) be a field
\[\phi: \zz \to K, \quad  1 \mapsto 1_K\]
It is a well-defined homomorphism, which invites a ideal of \(\zz\)
\[\ker \phi = \{n \in \zz   | n \cdot 1_k = 0_k\}\]
By the structure of \(\zz\), so there exists a integer \(p \in \zz\) such that \(\ker \phi = p \zz\),which gives the definition of the characteristic of the field.
\begin{definition}
  For any field \(K\), we define \(\mathrm{char}(K)\) be the characteristic of the field: either 0 or the smallest integer \(n \in \nn\) such that \(n\cdot1_k = 0\), by the natural map equivalently
  \[\begin{cases}
    \mathrm{char}(K) = 0 \iff  \mathrm{im}\phi \cong \zz \\
    \mathrm{char}(K) = p \iff  \mathrm{im}\phi \cong \zz/p\zz
  \end{cases}\]
\end{definition}
\textbf{The characteristic of a field is either zero or a prime number.} Suppose that \(\mathrm{char}(K) = p \neq 0\), if \(p =ab\) is not a prime, then we will get two zero divisor \(a\cdot1_K\) and \(b\cdot1_K\), but a field can not conatain any zero divisor, so \(p\) must be prime. An amazing thing in a field (or ring) with non-zero characteristic \(p\) is that we can write a equation
\[(x+y)^p = x^p + y^p\]
it has an interesting name: \textbf{freshman's dream}, this is an equality that would be written by someone who has studied very little mathematics or is just beginning to learn it.\\

Another natural map is \textbf{Frobenius endomorphism}, let \(K\) be a field with non-zero characteristic \(p\), then we define 
\[\sigma: K \to K, \quad x \mapsto x^p\]
It is a well-defined injective field homomorphism, so it is a endomorphism, but it is not necessary surjective.

\begin{example}
  Consier \(K = \ffp(x)\) a field of rational functions. \(\mathrm{char}(K) = p\) since \(\mathrm{im} \phi = \ffp\), and it is easy to observe that there exists no \(f \in \ffp(x)\) such that \(f^p(x) = x\), so the Frobenius map here is not surjective. 
\end{example}
A good case is finite field, in that case frobenius endomorphism is furthermore a automorphism, so the frobenius map will be in galois group and it reflects the structure of the finite field.

\subsection{Finite extension}

For the beginning, notice a classic isomorphism:
\[\rr[X]/(X-a) \cong \rr \]
For any \(a \in \rr\), and we review the isomorphism given in complex number field:
\[ \rr[X]/ (X^2+1) \cong \cc\]
Clearly, \(\cc\) is a larger field containning \(\rr\), but we wonder how we can get it from the algebra structure. Here it motivates us to consider the question from the polynoimal structure defined on a field. Firstly we need some lemmas, it is very clear after commutative ring:
\begin{lemma}
  Let \(L\) be a field containning \(K\) as a subfield, then \(L\) is a vector space over \(K\).
\end{lemma}

This lemma allows us to give a rough classification of field extension:
\begin{definition} 
  If \(L\) is a field containing \(K\) as a subfield.
  \begin{itemize}
    \item  \(L/K\) or \(K \subset L\) is denoted a \textbf{field extension} of \(K\).
    \item \(L/K\) is a \textbf{finite extension} if \(L\) is a finite-dimensional vector space over \(K\). The \textbf{degree} of the finite extension is defined as \([L:K] := \dim_K L\).
    \item \(a \in L\) is \textbf{algebraic} over \(K\) if there exists a non-zero polynomial \(f \in K[X]\) such that \(f(a) = 0\), \(L/K\) is an \textbf{algebraic extension} if every element in \(L\) is algebraic over \(K\).
    \item \(a\) is \textbf{transcendental} over \(K\) if it is not algebraic over \(K\).
  \end{itemize}
\end{definition}

Another lemma is the key to construction of field, the reason is that in a PID ring a ideal is maximal if and only if it is generated by an irreducible element.
\begin{lemma} \label{irr iff field}
  If \(K\) a field and \(p \in K[X]\), then \(p\) is irreducible if and only if \(K[X]/(p)\) is a field.
\end{lemma}


Hence the a contrsuction of field extension can be given by the quotient of polynoimal ring over a field by a good choice of ideal:
\begin{colorproposition}
  Let \(K\) be a field and \(I\) be a princiapl ideal generated by a monic irreducible polynoimal \(p \in K[X]\) of degree \(d\), Let \(L = K[X]/I\), then\\
  (1) \(L\) is a field and \(K\) can be embedded in \(L\) , so \(K\) can be identified as a subfield of \(L\).
  \\
  (2) \(p\) has a root \(\beta\) in \(L\), exactly \(\beta = X + I \in L\) \\
  (3) If \(g \in K[X]\) and \(\beta\) is a root of \(g\) in  \(L\), then \(p|g\).\\
  (4) \(p\) is the unique monic irreducible polynoimal in \(K[X]\) having \(\beta\) as a root in \(L\).\\
  (5) \(L\) is a finite extension of \(K\) of degree \(d\) with the basis \(\{1,\beta,...,\beta^{d-1}\}\).

\end{colorproposition}

  \begin{proof}
    (1) \(L\) is a field by above lemma, and we consider an embedding \(i(a) = a+I\) for any \(a \in K\), clearly it is injective and actually it is \(\pi|_K\), where \(\pi\) is the canoncial map from \(K[x]\) to \(K[x]/I\).

    (2) Suppose \(p(x) = a_0+...+a_dx^d \), then in \(L\), we have
    \begin{align*}
      p(\beta) & = a_0+a_1\beta+...+a_d\beta^d \\
      &= a_0+a_1(X+I)+...+a_d(X+I)^d \\
      &= a_0+a_1(X+I)+...+a_d(X^d+I) \\
      &= p(X)+I=I
    \end{align*}
    Here \(p(X) \in I\) and \(I\) is the zero element in \(L\).

    (3) If \(p\) does not divide \(g\), then \(gcd(p,g) = 1\) since \(p\) is irreducible, so PID ring \(K[X]\) imples the existence of \(r, t\) in \(K[X]\) such that
    \[1 = s(x)p(x)+t(x)g(x)\]
    put \(x=\beta\) in \(L\), then \(1 = 0\) leads to a contradiction.

    (4) immediately from (3). For (5) we use euclidean divison for polynoimal, any \(f \in K[X]\), there exists \(q ,r \in K[X]\) with \(\deg r < d\) such that \( f(x) = q(x)p(x)+r(x)\), then \(f+ I = r + I\) in \(L\), and if \(r(x) = b_0+...+b_kx^k\), \(k < d\), by opreations of ideal
    \[r+I = b_0 + b_1\beta+....+ b_k\beta^k\]
    so \(\{1,\beta,...,\beta^{d-1}\} \) spans \(L\). They are linearly independent because if we assume they are linearly dependent, that means there exists a polynoimal \(h \in K[X]\) of degree \(< d\) such that \(h\) has \(\beta\) as the root, but by (3) we know that \(p | h\), which leads to a contradiction since \(d= \deg p \leq \deg h \).
  \end{proof}

  Review the example in the beginning, the complex number field can also be constructed by adjoining element \(i\) to \(\rr\) such that \(i^2+1 = 0\), and we alaways write a complex number of the form \(a+ib\) with \(a,b\) real numbers, which is another construction of field extension, and more generally we have the following proposition:
\begin{proposition}
  Let \(K\) be a field and \(f \in K[X]\) irreducible, adjoin element \(c \notin K\) such that \(c\) is a root of \(f\), then \(K[c]\) is a field containning \(K\) as a subfield, and it can be written as \(K(c)\).
  \[K(c) = \{a_0+a_1c+...+a_kc^k| a_1,...,a_k \in K, k= \deg f-1\}\]
  \begin{proof}
    \(K[c]\) naturally is a ring with same identity with \(K\), so we just need to find the inverse. For any polynoimal \(p \in K[X]\) with degree less than \(\deg f\), it is co-prime with \(f\) since \(f\) is irreducible, so by Bezout's theorem for polynoimal, there exists \(r, t \in K[X]\) such that 
    \[p(x)r(x)+t(x)f(x) = 1\]
    hence we take \(x = c\) then immediately \(p(c)r(c) = 1\), so in \(K[c]\) we find the inverse of \(p(c)\). and notice that \(K[c]\) is a field garantee any rational polynoimal can have the form of polynoimal, so \(K[c] = K(c)\) evidently.
  \end{proof}
\end{proposition}

Here we find two method to extension the field, the first method is consider the quotient of polynomial ring, and we can embed \(K\) into it; the second is more direct, we just add some element in the field. 



\begin{colortheorem}[Structure of field extension]$ \\$
Let \(L/K\) be field extension of \(K\) and \(a \in L\) is algebraic, then
\\(1) There exists a unique \textbf{monic} irreducible polynoimal in \(K[X]\) having \(a\) as a root, formally we call it \textbf{minimal polynoimal} of \(a\) over \(K\), and denote it by \(\Pi_a\).
\\(2) If \(I = (\Pi_a)\), then there exists an isomorphism
\[\phi: K[X]/I \to K(a)\]
with \(X+I \mapsto a\) and \(c+I \mapsto c\) for all \(c \in K\).
\\(3) Let \(L'/K\) is another field extension and \(a' \in L'\) is also a root of \(\Pi_a\), then there exists an isomrphism \(\psi: K(a) \to K(a')\) such that \(\psi|_K = \mathrm{id}_K\) and \(\psi(a) = a'\).
\end{colortheorem}

\begin{proof}
  It needs to consider the \textbf{valuation map}, we define \(h:K[X] \to L\) by \(p \mapsto p(a)\), then clearly \(\ker h\) contains all polynoimals having \(a\) as a root, then notice that \(K[X]\) is a principal ideal ring, so \(\ker h\) must be the form \((p)\) with monic \(p \in K[X]\). By first isomorphism theorem, we know that \(K[X]/I \cong K(a)\), then by Lemma \ref{irr iff field} \(p\) must be irreducible, hence we prove (1) and (2), and we can draw commute diagram to finish (3):
\[\begin{tikzcd}
	{K[X]} && {K[a]} \\
	\\
	{K[a']} && {K[X]/I}
	\arrow["h", from=1-1, to=1-3]
	\arrow["{h'}"', from=1-1, to=3-1]
	\arrow["\pi"', from=1-1, to=3-3]
	\arrow["\simeq", from=3-3, to=1-3]
	\arrow["\simeq"', from=3-3, to=3-1]
\end{tikzcd}\]
where \(h'(p) = p(a')\), and \(\pi\) is the canoncial map. 
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
    The theorem shows a induction
    \[\{\text{algebraic elements in } L/K\} \leadsto \{\text{monic irreducible polynoimal over } K\}\]
    conversely it is not right since the field extension \(L\) may be not enough large, which refers the \textbf{"closure"} in the sense of algebraic element. For statement (3), a simple example is \(\qq(\sqrt{2}) = \qq(-\sqrt{2})\), and monic irreducible polynoimal \(X^2-2 \in \qq[X]\).
\end{remark}

Above is a typical example to motivate the definition of splitting field, and we can give a more general definition:

Suppose \(L/K\) is a extension and a polynoimal \(f \in K[X]\), we say \(f\) \textbf{splits} over \(K\) if there exists \(c,a_1,\dots,a_n \in K\) such that \(f\) can be factorized as linear terms:
\[f(x) = c(x-a_1)(x-a_2)\cdots(x-a_n)\]
above examples shows that a extension \(L/K\) can not always contain all roots of a polynomial, and we can construct a larger field.

    - As a finite extension, \([L:K] \leq n!\)
\end{theorem}

For the example at the begining, the experience of \(\cc\) gives us a hint that \(\qq(\sqrt[3]{2})\) can not contain the complex number \(\omega\), which reminds us to consider the roots of the polynomial in algebraic closure.
\begin{colordefinition}
  Let \(L/K\) be an algebraic extension, we define
  \[\Hom_{K-alg}(L,\bar{K}) = \{f:L \hookrightarrow \bar{K} \mid f|_K = id_K\}\]
  to be \textbf{\(K\)-embedding of \(L\)}.   
\end{colordefinition}

\begin{remark}
  It is natural to consider the category of field extensions of \(K\), we denote it by \(\mathbf{Ext}_K\). Its objects are the extension \(K \subset L\), or we can see it as a \(K\)-algebra by an embedding \(\tau:K \hookrightarrow L\). Hence its morphism is just the morhism of \(K\)-algebra, for example if we consider \(f:L \to L'\), then \(f\) must be injective since it is a morphism of field, and \(f(r) = rf(1) = r\) for any \(r \in K\), in particular \(L' = \bar{K}\) we get the definition of \(K\)-embedding of \(L\). Hence here we get a (non-full) subcategory of \(\mathbf{CAlg}_K\).
\end{remark}

For any \(L/\qq\) algebraic extension, we consider \(f\) is a embedding, then we have
\[ f(X^3-2) = f(X)^3 - 2 \qquad f(X^2+X+1) = f(X)^2+f(X)+1\]
for any \(X \in L\). which allows us to calculate that
\begin{align*}
  \Hom_{\qq-alg}(\qq(\sqrt[3]{2}),\bar{\qq}) = \{f_1,f_2,f_3\}
\end{align*}
with \(f_i(\sqrt[3]{2}) = \sqrt[3]{2} \omega^{i-1}\) for \(i=1,2,3\).
\[\Hom_{\qq-alg}(\qq(\sqrt[3]{2},\omega),\bar{\qq}) = \{f_{1,1},f_{1,2},f_{1,3},f_{2,1},f_{2,2},f_{2,3}\}\]
with \(f_{i,j}(\sqrt[3]{2}) = f_i(\sqrt[3]{2})\) and \(f_{i,j}(\omega) = \omega^j\) for \(i=1,2,3\) and \(j=1,2\). We can obeserve that the second case forms exactly a group since the image of \(f_{i,j}\) is \(\qq(\sqrt[3]{2},\omega)\) itself, and in particular it can be identified as \(S_3\) (notice \(f_{2,1}\) is of order 3 and \(f_{1,2}\) is of order 2, they are not commute).


\begin{colordefinition}
  Let \(L/K\) be a field extension, we define 
  \[\Gal(L/K)  = \Hom_{K-alg}(L,L) = \{ \sigma \in \Aut(L) \mid \sigma|_K = \id_K \} \]
  to be the \textbf{Galois group} (or \textbf{automorphism group}) of \(L/K\), it is a subgroup of \(\Aut(L)\) which fixes \(K\).
\end{colordefinition}

Notice a clear inclusion 
\[\Gal(L/K) \subset \Hom_{K-alg}(L,\bar{K})\]
which is suffisant to prove that when \(L = \qq(\sqrt[3]{2},\omega)\), the inclusion turns to be an equality. while \(L = \qq(\sqrt[3]{2})\), we can only get \(\Gal(L/\qq) = \{f_1\}\), hence the difference between Galois group and the embeddings is the key to distinguish the extensions.


\begin{colortheorem}
  Let \(L/K\) be a algebraic extension, then the following conditions are equivalent:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \(L\) is a splitting field of of a set of polynoimals in \(K[X]\).
    \item \(\Gal(L/K) = \Hom_K(L,\bar{K})\)
    \item For any \(a \in L\), the minimal polynomial \(\Pi_a \in K[X]\) splits over \(L\).
  \end{enumerate}
  If one of the above conditions is satisfied, we say \(L/K\) is a \textbf{normal extension}.
\end{colortheorem}

\begin{remark}
  Let \(a \in  L\) and \(\Pi_a\) is the minimal polynomial of \(a\), then \(K\)-embedding induces a bijection to the roots of \(\Pi_a\) in \(\bar{K}\), denote the set by \(R_a\)
  \[\Hom_{K}(L,\bar{K}) \to R_a, \quad f \mapsto f(a)\]
  exactly
\end{remark}




\subsection{Separable extension}
\begin{colorexample}
  Let us consider the function field \(K=\ff_p(t)\)
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \(X^p-t\) is irreducible in \(K[X]\) since \(K\) is the fraction field of \(\ff_p[t]\), and
    \[\ff_p[t][X] \cong \ff_p[t,X] \cong \ff_p[X,t]\]
    allow us to prove the irreducibility in \(\ff_p[t][X]\), so it holds in fraction field as well.
    \item consider \(L = K[X]/(X^p-t)\), then we can get a extension \(L\cong\ff_p(t^{1/p})\), which allows to factorize the minimal polynomial
    \[X^p - t = (X-t^{1/p})^p\]
    That shows \(L\) is a splitting field of \(X^p-t\) over \(K\), so \(L/K\) is a normal extension of degree \(p\).
    \item We exactly construct a extension \(L/K\) such that the minimal polynomial has repeated root, in this case the \(\bar{K}\)-embedding fails to distinguish the roots and \(\Gal(L/K) = \Hom_K(L,\bar{K}) = \{id\}\).
  \end{itemize}
  
\end{colorexample}


Review the case in \(\rr[X]\), although we do not prove the FTA exactly, but the our experience in \(\cc\) enough to conclude that any irreducible polynomial in \(\rr[X]\) can not have multiple roots in \(\cc\), since for the given distinct roots \(R = \{r_1,...,r_k\}\) with multiplicity \(\{m_1,...,m_k\}\), we can uniqely determine the monic polynomial of the form
\[\prod_{r_i \in \rr \cap R}(X-r_i)^{m_i} \cdot \prod_{\text{otherwise}}(X^2+b_jx+c_j)^{m_j}\]
since the complex roots always appear in pairs, so we can roughly conclude the result. Even more, we can not find a counterexample in \(\qq[X]\) as well, this section is to distinguish such a field.

\begin{definition}
  Let \(A\) be a \(k\)-algebra, an endomorphism \(\partial: A \to A\) is called a \textbf{derivation} if it satisfies the Leibniz rule:
  \[\forall f,g \in A, \quad \partial(fg) = \partial(f)g + f \partial(g) \quad \]
  It is easy to verify that
  \begin{align*}
    &\partial(\lambda) = 0, \quad \forall \lambda \in k \\
    &\partial(f^n) = nf^{n-1}\partial(f), \quad \forall f \in A, n \in \nn
  \end{align*}
\end{definition}
We can use the derivation to define the formal derivative of a polynoimal, which is a good view that does not depend on the the exactly form of the polynoimal.
\[\Omega_{L/K}=0 \iff L/K \text{ is separable}\]
This is the general explanation of the separable extension by Kähler differentials. Exactly in the definition we take \(A = k[X]\), and then endomorphism will be determined by the image of \(X\) by the universal property of polynomial ring, then we can get the formal derivative of any polynoimal
\begin{definition}
  Let \(\partial(X) = 1\), then for any \(f = \sum_n a_nX^n \in k[X]\), we can define the formal derivative of \(f\) by
  \[f'(X) := \partial(f) = \sum_n na_nX^{n-1}\]
  and a polynoimal \(f\) is called \textbf{separable} if \(f\) is coprime with its formal derivative \(f'\) in \(k[X]\).
\end{definition}

Notice that \(k[X]\) is a PID, so the coprime condition is equivalent to the existence of \(r, s \in k[X]\) such that
\[1 = r(X)f(X) + s(X)f'(X)\]
we describle the separable polynomial without talking about roots of polynoimal, and similarly we can give a definition of roots of a polynoimal via arithemtic of polynoimal:
\begin{definition}
  Let \(f \in k[X]\) and \(a \in k\), then \(a\) is a \textbf{root} of \(f\) if there exists \(k \in \nn^*\)such that
  \[(X-a)^n \mid f(X)\]
  and the \textbf{order of the root} is defined as the largest integer \(m\) such that
  \[(X-a)^m \mid f(X) \land (X-a)^{m+1} \nmid f(X)\]
  in particular, \(a\) is a \textbf{simple root} if the order of the root is 1, and it is a multiple root if the order of the root is greater than 1.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
  Following this definition, \(f(a) = 0\) if \(a\) is a root of \(f\), the coverse is not right in general, the ring should at least be an integral domain \textbf{(sure for field)} or the polynoimal should be monic, the proof depends on the division of polynoimal.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}
  For polynoimal \(f \in k[X]\), the following conditions are equivalent:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \(f\) is separable.
    \item \(f\) has no multiple root in \(\bar{k}\).
    \item \(f\) and \(f'\) has no common root in a algebraic closure \(\bar{k}\) of \(k\).
    \item \(f\) has exactly \(\deg f\) distinct roots in \(\bar{k}\).
    \item \(\bar{k}[X]/(f)\) is reduced and isomorphic to \(\bar{k}^{\deg f}\).
  \end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  (1) \(\Rightarrow\) (2): If \(f\) has a multiple root \(a\), then \((X-a)^2 \mid f(X)\), so \(f(X) = (X-a)^2g(X)\) for some \(g \in k[X]\), then we can calculate the formal derivative of \(f\):
  \[f'(X) = 2(X-a)g(X)+(X-a)^2g'(X)\]
  hence we get \((X-a)|f(X)\) and \((X-a)|f'(X)\), which contradicts the coprime condition.

  (2) \(\Rightarrow\) (3): in \(\bar{k}\), we can factorized \(f\) as linear terms, and the formal derivative of \(f\) can be caculated of the form
  \[f'(X) = \sum_{a} \frac{f(X)}{X-a}\]
  where \(a\) runs over all roots of \(f\), we suppose that \(b\) is a common root of \(f'\), then \(f'(b) = f(X)/(X-b)|_{b=0} = 0 \) implies that \(X-b\) divides \(f(X)/-b\), which gives a contradiction.

  (3) \(\Rightarrow\) (4): similar argument as above.

  (4) \(\Rightarrow\) (5): The isomorphism can be proved by the\textbf{ chinese remainder theorem}, and the product of fields is reduced surely.

  (5) \(\Rightarrow\) (4): Notice that \(\bar{k}[X]/(X-a)^2\) is not reduced with \(\overline{X-a}^2 = 0 \) in the quotient, and for any \(f\) we can factorized it to get a isomorphism
  \[\bar{k}[X]/(f) \cong \prod_{a } \bar{k}[X]/(X-a)^{m_a}\]
  with \(a\) distinct roots of \(f\) and \(m_a\) the multiplicity, so surely \(m_a\) should be all \(1\).

  (4) \(\Rightarrow\) (1): caculation.
\end{proof}

actually we can characterize the irreducible polynomial if it is separable, it depends on the characteristic of the field.

\begin{proposition}
  Let \(f\) be an irreducible polynomial in \(k[X]\), then \(f\) is inseparable if and only if \(f' = 0\), which shows that \\
  - If \(\mathrm{Char}(k) = 0\), any irreducible polynomial is separable.\\
  - If \(\mathrm{Char}(k) =p\), any irreducible polynomial is of the form \(g(X^{p^r})\) with \(g\) a separable irreducible polynomial and \(r \in \nn\), and the representation is unique.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  In \(k[X]\) (a UFD), \(f\) and \(f'\) coprime means their gcd associates to \(1\), or equivalently the gcd is a unit. When \(f\) is irreducible, then \(f\) is a prime element in \(k[X]\), so the gcd is either \(f\) or a unit. If \(f\) is inseparable, then the gcd is \(f\), so \(f|f'\) implies \(f' = 0\) since \(\deg f' < \deg f\). 

\end{proof}

\begin{colordefinition}
  Let \(L/K\) be a algebraic extension, then \(L/K\) is called a \textbf{separable extension} if for any \(a \in L\), the minimal polynomial \(\Pi_a\) of \(a\) over \(K\) is separable.
\end{colordefinition}

\begin{remark}
  \textbf{Any subextension of a separable extension is separable}: Let \(K \subset E \subset L\) be a tower of algebraic extension, and \(L/K\) is separable. If \(a \in E\), then \(\Pi_{a,E}\) divides \(\Pi_{a,L}\), and factor of separable polynoimal is still separable (verify).

\subsection{Galois extension}

Let \(K/k\) be a finite extension, with \(\Aut(K/k)\) the automorphism group then we can consider the group action \(\Aut(K/k) \circlearrowleft K\) defined by
\[\sigma \cdot a : = \sigma(a)\]
the fixed point of the action will be remarkable:

\begin{lemma}
  Let \(S \subset \Aut(K/k)\) be a non-empty subset, then
  \[K^S := \{x \in K \mid  \sigma(x) = x \text{ for all } \sigma \in S\}\]
  is a intermediate field of \(K/k\).
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \(\sigma \in \Aut(K/k)\) fixes \(k\), so \(k \subset K^S\). For any \(x,y \in K^S\) and \(\sigma \in S\), we can calculate
\[\sigma(x+y) = \sigma(x) + \sigma(y) = x+y\]
\[\sigma(xy) = \sigma(x)\sigma(y) = xy\]
it is enough to show the reuslt.
\end{proof}




\begin{colortheorem}
  Let \(K/k\) be a finite extension, then the following conditions are equivalent:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \(K/k\) is normal and separable.
    \item For any \(a \in K\), the minimal polynomial of \(a\) splits over \(K\) with \[\Pi_a(X) = \prod_{\sigma \in \Aut(K/k)}(X - \sigma(a))\]
    \item \(K\) is a splitting field of a separable polynomial in \(k[X]\).
    \item \(|\Aut(K/k)| = [K:k]\)
    \item Fixed field \(K^{\Aut(K/k)} = k\). 
  \end{enumerate}
\end{colortheorem}

\begin{proof}
  (1) implies (2): Since \(K/k\) is normal, then \(\Aut(K/k)\) is just the \(k\)-embedding onto itself, that means \(\sigma(a)\) runs over all roots of \(\Pi_a\) in \(\bar{k}\) exactly, and since \(K/k\) is separable, the roots are of one-multiplicity, so \(Pi_a\) splits like the form in (2).

  (2) implies (3): \(\Pi_a\) is a separable polynomial in \(K[X]\) and splits over \(K\), so we can consider the splitting field of all minimal polynomials.

  (3) implies (1), it is immediate.

  (3) implies (4): Since (1) and (3) are equivalent, so it is enough to show that (1) implies (4), and it can be proved by cardinal
  \[|\Aut(K/k)| = |\Hom(K,\bar{k})| = [K:k]\]
  The first equality holds since \(K/k\) is normal, and the second equality holds since \(K/k\) is separable.

  (4) implies (5): Set \(k' = K^{\Aut(K/k)}\), then we can prove that \(\Aut(K/k') = \Aut(K/k)\), and then we can prove that \(k' = k\) by proving that 
  \[[K:k'] \leq [K:k] = |\Aut(K/k)| = |\Aut(K/k')| \leq [K:k']\]
  the first inequality holds since \(k \subset k'\), and the last inequality holds since for any finite extension \(K/k\) we have
  \[\Aut(K/k) \subset \Hom(K,\bar{k}), \quad |\Hom(K,\bar{k})| \leq [K:k]\]

  (5) implies (1): For any \(a \in K\), we can set consider the orbit of \(a\), it contains the distinct roots of \(\Pi_a\) in \(K\), and then we set \(f(X) = \prod_{r \in Orb(a)}(X-r)\). For any \(\sigma \in \Aut(K/k)\), we can view that \(\sigma(f) = f\) since \(\sigma\) just permutes the roots
  \[\sigma(f) = \prod_{r \in Orb(a)}(X-\sigma(r)) = \prod_{r \in Orb(\sigma(a))}(X-r) = f\]
   that means any coefficient of \(f\) will lie in \(K^{\Aut(K/k)} = k\), i.e. \(f \in k[X]\). Hence \(\Pi_a\) divides \(f\) since \(f(a) = 0\), and \(\Pi_a\) is separable since \(f\) is separable. So we can actually show (5) implies (3), and (3) implies (1) as before.
\end{proof}

The equivalent statement motivates us to give the definition:

\begin{definition}
  An algebraic extension \(K/k\) is called a \textbf{Galois extension} if it is normal and separable, and in this case we call \(\Aut(K/k)\) the \textbf{Galois group} of the extension, and denote it by \(\Gal(K/k)\).

Another \textbf{alternative definition} of galois extension is an algebraic extension \(K/k\) such that \(K^{\Aut(K/k)} = k\), which is equivalent to the definition above by the theorem without limitation on the finiteness of the extension.

\end{definition}

\begin{colorproposition}
  Let \(K/k\) be a galois extension with \(L\) a intermediate field, then 
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \(K/L\) is a galois extension.
    \item \(\Gal(K/L)\) a is subgroup of \(\Gal(K/k)\), if extension \(K/k\) is finite, then the index of galois group is \([L:k]\).
    \item For any \(\sigma \in \Gal(K/k)\), we have conjugation
    \[\Gal(K/\sigma(L)) = \sigma \Gal(K/L) \sigma^{-1}\]
    \item \(L/k\) is galois if and only if \(\Gal(K/L)\) is a normal subgroup of \(\Gal(K/k)\), and in this case we have an isomorphism
    \[\Gal(L/k) \cong \Gal(K/k)/\Gal(K/L)\]
  \end{enumerate}
\end{colorproposition}

\begin{proof}
  (1) For any \(a \in K\), Let \(\Pi_a \in k[X]\) be the minimal polynomial of \(a\) over \(k\), and \(M_a \in L[X]\) be the minimal polynomial of \(a\) over \(L\), then \(M_a \) divides \(\Pi_a\), it implies that \(M_a\) is separable since \(\Pi_a\) is separable. What's more, the roots of \(M_a\) are also the roots of \(\Pi_a\), and \(\Pi_a\) takes all roots in \(K\), so does \(M_a\), hence \(K/L\) is normal amd separable.

  (2) Since \(k \subset L\), hence \(Gal(K/L)\subset Gal(K/k)\), and it is not hard to verify it is exactly a subgroup, for index we can conclude that
  \[[\Gal(K/k):\Gal(K/L)] = [K:k]/[K:L] = [L:k]\]
  The first equality holds since the two extensions are galois, and the second holds by tower law.

  (3) For any \(\sigma\) we have

  \begin{align*}
    f \in \Gal(K/\sigma(L)) &\iff f(\sigma(x)) = \sigma(x) , \quad \forall x \in L \\
    &\iff \sigma^{-1}\circ f \circ \sigma|_{L} = id_L \\
    &\iff \sigma^{-1}\circ f \circ \sigma \in \Gal(K/L) \\
    &\iff f \in \sigma \Gal(K/L) \sigma^{-1}
  \end{align*}

  (4) If \(L/k\) is galois, then \(\Gal(L/k) = \Hom_{k}(L,\bar{k})\) since it is normal, hence for any \(\sigma \in \Gal(K/k)\), when consider \(\sigma|_{L}\) as the restriction map, it will be contained in the \(k\)-embedding of \(L\), so \(\sigma(L) = L\), which shows that the following map is well-defined
  \[\Gal(K/k) \to \Gal(L/k), \quad \sigma \mapsto \sigma|_{L}\]
  the kenel of the map is exactly \(\Gal(K/L)\), so it is enough to prove it is surjective.

  Conversely, if \(\Gal(K/L)\) is normal, then for any \(\sigma \in \Gal(K/k)\) we have
  \[\sigma(L) = K^{\Gal(K/\sigma(L))} = K^{\sigma\Gal(K/L)\sigma^{-1}} = K^{\Gal(K/L)} = L\]
  so again above map is well-defined and surjective, so any \(\tau \in Gal(L/k)\), there exists \(\sigma \in \Gal(K/k)\) such that \(\tau(L) = \sigma|_{L}(L) = L\), which shows that \(L/k\) is normal, so it is galois as well since the sub-extension of a separable extension is separable.


\end{proof}
