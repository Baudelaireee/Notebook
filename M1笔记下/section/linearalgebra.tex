\subsection{Semi-direct product}

\begin{definition}
    Let \(G\) and \(H\) be groups, and let \(\varphi: H \to \Aut(G)\) be a group homomorphism. The \textbf{semi-direct product} \(G \rtimes_\varphi H\) is the set \(G \times H\) with the multiplication defined by
    \[(g_1, h_1)(g_2, h_2) = (g_1 \varphi(h_1)(g_2), h_1 h_2)\]
    for all \(g_1, g_2 \in G\) and \(h_1, h_2 \in H\).

\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
    If \(\varphi\) is trival, i.e. \(\varphi(h) = \mathrm{id}_G\) for all \(h \in H\), then the semi-direct product \(G \rtimes_\varphi H\) is just the \textbf{direct product} \(G \times H\).

    Conversely, if \(G \rtimes H\) is abelian, then \(\varphi\) must be trivial by caculating \((1,h)(g,1) = (g,1)(1,h)\), since it will be \(\varphi(h)(g) = g\) for any \(h\) and \(g\).

\end{remark}

\end{lemma}

\begin{definition}
    A group \(G\) is an inner semi-direct product of \(N\) and \(H\) if \(N \vartriangleleft G\), \(H < G\) and \(G = NH\) with \(N \cap H = \{e\}\).
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
    The definition implies that the group has semi-direct product structure, and the automorphism comes from the inner automorphism:
    \[G \cong N \rtimes_{\varphi} H, \quad\]
    where \(\varphi: H \to \Aut(N)\) is defined by \(\varphi(h)(n) = hnh^{-1}\), it is well-defined since \(N\) is normal. To prove the isomorphism, we set \(f:N \rtimes H \to G\) by \(f(n,h) = nh\), then it is surjective since \(NH = G\), and the it is indeed a morphism
    \begin{align*}
        f((n_1,h_1)(n_2,h_2)) &= f(n_1 \varphi(h_1)(n_2), h_1 h_2)\\
        &= n_1 h_1 n_2 h_1^{-1} h_1 h_2\\
        &= n_1 h_1 n_2 h_2\\
        &= f(n_1,h_1)f(n_2,h_2)
    \end{align*}
    So it is enough to check the injectivity: if \(f(n,h) = e\), then \(n = h^{-1} \in N \cap H\), thus \(n = e\) and \(h = e\).

\end{remark}


\begin{proposition}
    Let \(G \rtimes H\) be a semi-direct product with respect to \(\varphi: H \to \Aut(G)\), then \(G\) can be idetified as  a normal subgroup of \(G \rtimes H\) while \(H\) can be identified as a subgroup of \(G \rtimes H\), and we have a splitting exact sequence
\[0 \to G \to G \rtimes H \to H \to 0\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    We consider the inclusion \(i:G \to G \rtimes H\) defined by \(i(g) = (g,1)\) and the projection \(\pi:G \rtimes H \to H\) defined by \(\pi(g,h) = h\), then we can verify  that \(i\) and \(\pi\) are homomorphisms, and \(\ker \pi = i(G) \cong G\), so it shows that \(G\) can be identified as a normal subgroup of \(G \rtimes H\) and the sequence is exact. Finally, to prove that the sequence splits, we define a section \(s:H \to G \rtimes H\) by \(s(h) = (1,h)\), then it is easy to check that \(s\) is a injective hormomorphism such that \(\pi \circ s = id_H\), it shows that the sequence splits, and \(H\) can be identified as \(s(H)\).
\end{proof}

\subsection{Minimal polynomial}

\begin{lemma}
    Let \(f:V \to V\) be a linear endomorphism with \(V\) a finite-dimensional vector space over \(k\), and \(\mathcal{V} = \{v_1,\dots,v_n\}\) be a basis of \(V\) such that \(A\) is the matrix of \(f\) with respect to \(\mathcal{V}\), then the minimal polynomial of the matrix  \(A\) is exactly the minimal polynomial of the endomorphism \(f\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    We consider the twist map \(\varphi : k^n \to V\) defined by \(\varphi(e_i) = v_i\), where \(e_i\) is the standard basis of \(k^n\), then we have the following commutative diagram:

	\arrow["A"', from=1-1, to=2-1]
	\arrow["f", from=1-2, to=2-2]

    thus for any polynomial \(p \in k[X]\), we have \(p(f)\circ \varphi = \varphi \circ p(A)\), hence \(f\) will have the same minimal polynomial as \(A\).

    We can consider two representations of \(k[X]\)-module by
    \[\rho:k[X] \to \End(V), \quad X \mapsto f\]

    then \(\varphi\) gives an isomorphism of two representations.
\end{remark}



\subsection{Decomposition of endomorphisms}


\begin{colortheorem}[Real-Polar decomposition] $ \\$
For any real endomorphism \(A \in \mathrm{GL}_n( \rr)\), there exists a unique orthogonal endomorphism \(O \in O(n)\) and a unique symmetric positive definite endomorphism \(P \in S^{++}_n(\rr)\) such that
\[A = OP\]
\end{colortheorem}

\begin{proof}
    Here is the outline of the proof:\\
    (1) A \textbf{lemma} about the existence of square root are needed here: If \(A \in S^+_n(\rr)\), then there exists a (unique) \(B \in S^+_n(\rr)\) such that \(B^2 = A\). \\
    (2) Prove that \(A^tA\) is (semi-definite) positive, thus by the lemma there exist a (unique) squar root \(P = \sqrt{A^tA}\), and we prove that \(P\) is positive definite. \\
    (3) Define \(O = AP^{-1}\), and prove that \(O\) is orthogonal.\\
    (4) Prove the uniqueness of the decompoistion.\\

    Here is the details:\\
    (1) By real-spectral theorem, there exists \(O \in O(n)\) such that
    \[A = ODO^T, \quad D = \mathrm{dig}(\lambda_1,...,\lambda_n)\]
    where \(\lambda_i \geq 0\) are the eigenvalues of \(A\), and we set \(\Sigma = \mathrm{dig}(\sqrt{\lambda_1},...,\sqrt{\lambda_n})\) such that
    \[A = (O\Sigma O^T)^2\]
    which shows that \(B = O \Sigma O^T\) is a (unique) square root of \(A\) and it is positive since \(\sqrt{\lambda_i} \geq 0\). 

    (2) \(A^T A\) is clearly symmetric, and it is positive since
    \[x^T (A^T A) x = \|Ax\| \geq 0\]
    for any \(x \in \rr^n\) under the common inner product, and \(A\) is invertible such that \(\|Ax\| = 0\) if and only if \(x = 0\), thus \(A^TA\) is positive definite, so does its square root (the eignevalues is strictly positive).

    (3) \(P^{-1} = (P^{-1})^T\) since \(P\) is symmetric, so we have
    \[O^T O = (P^{-1})^T A^T A P^{-1} = (P^{-1})^T P^2 P^{-1} = I\]

    (4) Suppose that there eixsts another decomposition \(A = O_0P_0\), then we have
    \[A^TA = P_0^T O_0^T O_0 P_0 = P_0^2\]
    so \(P_0\) is the positive square root of \(A^TA\), if the uniqueness of (1) is proved, we can finish the proof, but here we give another proof of the uniqueness only under the existence of the square root: we have \(O_0^{-1}O = P_0P^{-1}\), so it is easy to check that \(P_0P^{-1}\) is \textbf{orthogonal}; notice that \(P_0\) and \(A^TA\) commute, and there exists a polynomial \(f\) such that
    \[f(A^TA) = P\]
    it can be done by using lagrange interpolation, thus \(P_0\) and \(P\) commute as two symmetric matrix, then \(P\) and \(P_0\) can be diagonalized simultaneously by one orthogonal \(H\) such that
    \[P = HDH^T \quad P_0 = H D_0 H^T\]
    where \(D = \mathrm{dig}(\lambda_1,...,\lambda_n)\) and \(D_0 = \mathrm{dig}(\mu_1,...,\mu_n)\), thus
    \[P_0P^{-1} = HD_0^{-1}DH^T\]
    it is \textbf{symmetric and positive definite} since \(D^{-1}_0 D\) is digonal with positive entries, thus \(P_0P^{-1} \in O(n) \cap S^{++}_n(\rr) = \{I\}\), which implies that \(P_0 = P\) and \(O_0 = O\).
\end{proof}

\begin{colortheorem}[Complex-Polar decomposition] $ \\$
    For any complex endomorphism \(A \in \mathrm{GL}_n( \cc)\), there exists a unique unitary endomorphism \(U \in U(n)\) and a unique hermitian positive definite endomorphism \(P \in H^{++}_n(\cc)\) such that
    \[A = UP\]
\end{colortheorem}

The proof is similar to the real case, we set \(P = \sqrt{A^*A}\) and \(U = AP^{-1}\), and the lemma is based on the complex-spectral theorem for normal opreator: The postive Hermitian endomorphism has a unique positive Hermitian square root.

\end{remark}

\subsection{Lattice}

\begin{definition}
    Let \(V\) be a \(\rr\)-vector space of dimension \(n\)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
    Let \(V\) be a \(\rr\)-vector space of dimension \(n\), a parallelotope of \(V\) with respect to a basis \(\{v_1, \dots, v_n\}\) is the set of points
    \[P = \{\sum_{i = 1}^n c_iv_i \mid c_i \in [0,1)\}\]
    The volume of \(P\) corresponds to the an inner product \(\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle\) is defined by
    \[\mathrm{Vol}(P) = \sqrt{\det G(v_1,\dots,v_n)}\]
    where \(G(v_1,\dots,v_n)\) is the Gram matrix with entries \(G_{ij} = \langle v_i, v_j \rangle\).
\end{definition}

The definition is resonable with the following reasons:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Let \(P\) be of dimension 3 with respect to the euclidean inner product, then the volume of \(P\) is given by the triple product
    \[\mathrm{Vol}(P) = |v_1\cdot(v_2\times v_3)| = |\det [v_1, v_2, v_3]|\]
    with respect to the standard basis.

    \item Let \(\{v_i\}_i\) be an orthogonal basis, then the Gram matrix \(G = I_n\) exactly, hence the volume of a hypercube is 1, which same as what we do in plane geometry and solid geometry.
    
    \item 
\end{enumerate}

with the convention above, it allows us to construct the invrariant measure on any real inner product space.

\begin{proposition}
    For any real inner product space \(V\) of dimension \(n\), there exists a natural isometry (\(\rr^n\) equipped with the standard inner product) 
    \[\Phi: \rr^n \to V, \quad (x_1,\dots,x_n) \mapsto \sum_{i=1}^n x_ie_i\]
    where \(\{e_1,\dots,e_n\}\) is an orthonormal basis of \(V\), then \(\mu = \lambda \circ \Phi^{-1}\) defines a Haar measure on \(V\) such that for any parallelotope \(P\) we have
    \(\mu(P) = \mathrm{Vol}(P) \) exactly.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    \(\mu\) is exactly a pushforward measure, to verify that it is a Haar measure, we need to check the translation invariance: for any \(v \in V\) and any measurable set \(S \subset V\), we have
    \[\mu(v+S) = \lambda(\Phi^{-1}(v+S)) = \lambda(\Phi^{-1}(v) + \Phi^{-1}(S)) = \lambda(\Phi^{-1}(S)) = \mu(S)\]
    where the second equality is due to the linearity of \(\Phi\) and the third equality is due to the translation invariance of the Lebesgue measure \(\lambda\). Now we consider the parallelotope \(P\) with respect to a basis \(\{v_1,\dots,v_n\}\) and set \(\Phi(a_i) = v_i\), then we have
    \[\mu(P) = \lambda (\Phi^{-1}(P)) = |\det A| = \sqrt{\det A^tA}\]
    where \(A\) is the matrix with columns \(a_i\), and isometry shows that \(A^TA\) is exactly the Gram matrix \(G(v_1,\dots,v_n)\), which finishes the proof.
\end{proof}

\section{Representation of finite group}

The representation theory appears in almost all branches of mathematics, but the basic ideas and techniques are quite similar, among them the representation theory of finite groups is the most basic one to start with.

\begin{definition}
    Let \(G\) be a finite group and \(k\) be a field, \(\mathbf{Rep}_k(G)\) is a category definied as following:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The objects is of the form \((V,\rho)\), where \(V\) is a \(k\)-vector space such that \(\rho\) a group homomorphism 
    \[\rho : G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)\]
    such a objects is called a \textbf{\(k\)-representation of \(G\)}.

    \item A morphism from \((V,\rho)\) to \((W,\tau)\) is a \(k\)-linear map \(T \in \mathcal{L}(V,W)\) such that for any \(g \in G\) we have the following commutative diagram:
    % https://q.uiver.app/#q=WzAsNCxbMCwwLCJWIl0sWzEsMCwiVyJdLFswLDEsIlYiXSxbMSwxLCJXIl0sWzAsMSwiVCJdLFsyLDMsIlQiXSxbMCwyLCJcXHJobyhnKSIsMl0sWzEsMywiXFx0YXUoZykiXV0=
\[\begin{tikzcd}
	V & W \\
	V & W
	\arrow["T", from=1-1, to=1-2]
	\arrow["{\rho(g)}"', from=1-1, to=2-1]
	\arrow["{\tau(g)}", from=1-2, to=2-2]
	\arrow["T", from=2-1, to=2-2]
\end{tikzcd}\]
    such a morphism is called an \textbf{intertwiner} or a \textbf{\(G\)-equivariant}. The isomorphisms in the category is just a linear isomorphism such that above diagram commutes.

    \item In fact under the composition of map, the morphisms is well-defined and associative, and the identity inside a representation \((V,\rho)\) is the identity map \(T(x) = x\) for any \(x \in V\), thus it is indeed a category.
    
    \item A representation is called faithful if \(\rho\) is injective, and it is called trivial if \(\rho(g) = id_V\) for any \(g \in G\).
    
    \item If \(V\) is finite-dimensional over \(k\), then a representation \((V,\rho)\) is called finite-dimensional, and the dimension of \(V\) is called the dimension of the representation.


\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

It is the basic definition of representation of groups, so firstly we need to describle more structures of the objects and morphisms such that we can know more about what the category exactly looks like.

\begin{definition}
    Let \(W \subset V\) as a subspace, we say that \(W\) is invariant under the representation \((V,\rho)\) if for any \(g \in G\) we have
    \[\rho(W) \subset W\]
    and we define \((W, \rho|_{W})\) as a subrepresentation of \((V,\rho)\). There always exists two trivial subrepresentations: \((\{0\},\rho|_{\{0\}})\) and \((V,\rho)\) itself.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
    The definition is exactly natural, in the sense of morphism, subrepresentation is just a representation can be embedded into a larger representation by a injective intertwiner, i.e. if \((W,\tau)\) a representation with \(W \subset V\) as a subspace, then the inclusion \(i: V \to W\) will be an intertiwner such that
    \[\tau(w) = \tau(g)(i(w)) = i(\rho(w)) = \rho(w)\]
    by the definition of \(\tau\), then we can recover the definition of invariant.
\end{remark}

\textcolor{blue}{As we study groups by classifying their subgroups, we can also study representation by considering their subrepresentations, so it motivates us to consider a special case, which is analogous to simple groups.
}

\begin{definition}
    A representation \((V,\rho)\) is called irreducible if it has no non-trivial subrepresentation, i.e. the only subrepresentations are \((\{0\},\rho|_{\{0\}})\) and \((V,\rho)\) itself.
\end{definition}
